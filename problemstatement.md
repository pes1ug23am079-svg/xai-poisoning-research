Problem Statement
Machine learning models deployed in high-stakes settings — fraud detection, medical triage, content moderation — are increasingly accompanied by post-hoc explanation methods like SHAP and LIME to satisfy transparency requirements. These explanations are trusted by practitioners to reflect what the model has learned.
But this trust rests on an untested assumption: that explanations remain faithful when the training data has been compromised.
Data poisoning attacks — where an adversary injects malicious or mislabeled samples into the training set — are a known threat to model accuracy. What is not known is what happens to explanations. Does a poisoned model produce explanations that look normal while the model itself has been corrupted? Or do explanations degrade visibly, potentially serving as an early warning signal before accuracy drops enough to trigger alarms?
This is not a theoretical concern. In practice, models are often retrained on continuously collected data where label quality cannot be guaranteed — crowdsourced annotations, user-generated feedback, scraped web data. Poisoning in these settings is not always deliberate; it can be structural (systematic labeling bias, distribution shift in incoming data). In either case, the question is the same: can we trust the explanation when we can't fully trust the data?
Current XAI research evaluates explanation faithfulness exclusively on clean data. There is no benchmark, no established metric, and no empirical characterization of how faithfulness degrades under poisoning. This gap means that explanation-based auditing systems — increasingly used in regulated industries — may be producing false assurance: explanations that appear coherent while the underlying model has quietly learned corrupted decision boundaries.
